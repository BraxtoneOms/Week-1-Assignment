FTC Cracks Down on “Made in USA” Label Misuse: Warning Letters and Legal Risk

Date: July 29, 2025

Summary

In a sweeping enforcement action this past week, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) escalated scrutiny of companies using "Made in the USA" claims. Under the 2021 MUSA Labeling Rule, such claims may now trigger fines of up to $53,088 per violation if products do not meet the "all or virtually all" domestic origin standard. That means final assembly, processing, and all significant components must originate in the U.S.
The FTC issued formal warning letters to makers across multiple sectors including flagpoles, footwear brands, and personal care products citing possible deceptive marketing practices under both the FTC Act and labeling regulations. The agency’s definition of "label" now explicitly includes online marketing and images, significantly broadening enforcement reach.
At the same time, private litigants and self-regulatory bodies have intensified legal challenges. Class-action lawsuits addressing misleading "Made in USA" claims doubled in 2025, with several high-profile cases in California invoking state vs federal labeling law disputes. The National Advertising Division (NAD) has also issued decisions against companies overstating domestic content.

Why It Matters

  1. Risk of heavy penalties: Companies face both federal enforcement and private lawsuits.
  2. Compliance complexity: Firms must audit supply chains to ensure genuine U.S. sourcing.
  3. Marketing vigilance: Claims, including those made online must truthfully reflect product origins.

What businesses should do:

  1. Review supply chain documentation to confirm “Made in USA” eligibility.
  2. Update marketing materials—including digital and packaging labels to comply.
  3. Consult legal or regulatory experts, especially if under scrutiny or litigation.

As scrutiny around domestic origin claims grows, companies claiming U.S. manufacturing credentials are urged to dial in accuracy missteps may prove costly both legally and reputationally.
